My favorite exercise is trail walking / running, usually on fairly rough terrain. Often I find myself wondering if I'm being efficient, by which I mean using the least amount of energy. I'm sure there is all kinds of science behind this question, and I'll reference what I find here. However, I'm going to start out with pure speculation:

Regardless of going uphill, flat, or downhill, you want to minimize acceleration of (1) your center of mass **, (2) of your feet and (3) your tendons / muscles.

** You naturally have a 'pendulum' motion (actually a curtate cycloid) from walking which is more effcient than trying to keep your center of mass completely still (thanks evolution). This is both up-down and side-to-side due to hip rotation and general mechanics (here). So, for (1) we want to subtract out that natural motion and not include it in the thesis. Trying to minimize that natural motion will actually increase your energy use.

I'm going to look into the science to see if this is supported, but first a bit more detail on the speculation. I'll asssume that you want to travel at a reasonable pace. Obviously, sitting on a rock and taking infinite time to complete a hike uses very little energy; it's also not very productive.

Acceleration is a change in speed or direction. So, changes in acceleration include going faster or slower, but also changing your angle of accent or descent, moving left or right, etc. If you could do a hike in a perfectly straight line at a constant incline with no need to raise your foot higher than the next step requires, you would be minimizing your overall acceleration. If, on such a route, your muscles and tendons contract and extend the least possible to move you, that also minimizes acceleration. Simply thinking of acceleration as 'change,' you want to minimize the number of changes you make.

  • Stepping over, versus around, rocks.
  • Taking larger vertical step versus several smaller verticals.
  • Walking on a sloped beach; narrow your gait to compensate?
  • Running switchbacks; clean arcs at the corners (longer route, maintain speed) or sharp turns (shorter, lose speed)

In all of these situations, given your brain expends a lot of energy, are you better to simply let your animal brain (lower energy use) make the decisions...or is the energy used in higher thinking functions (lots of neurons firing) offset by the gains realized from smarter decisions? Let's try to put this one to bed. According to this , the brain uses about 20% of your energy, but it's energy use does not change very much based on what you are thinking. Most of that brain energy goes towards maintenance of the brain, not higher or lower processing functions. In fact, with very high concentration tasks (those red areas in an fMRI) your brain energy only goes up by 8%, to 21.6% of total energy use. In the meantime, muscle energy can chhange by a factor of three or four, between sitting still and moving aggresssively. So, it is almost certain that if you can make better decisions about your hiking by concentrating on it, that energy will be more than offset by efficient muscle usage.

Basics from what I've read so far... Nothing that completely refutes my thesis, but lots more work to do.

For the sprints, it takes longer to do 100M in 10M segments. Even taking into account resting metabolism after a straight line sprint, I expect the energy/time will be higher for the stops/starts. Since I'm most interested in longer ventures, perhaps I need to look at energy/30 minutes, versus energy in very small time periods>

Metabolic cost of velocity Energy used grows linearly with speed over a wide range of speeds. Metabolic cost of Change of Direction (CoD) Changing direction takes more energy than just running straight.
Metabolic cost of transport (MCOT) We all have a natural gait, and it is our most efficient; however, small fluctuations don't have a large impact. "The current study aimed to characterize the interaction between net MCOT and step-to-step variability while walking at different speeds. The amount of step length variations was linearly associated with MCOT, likely because increases in variations are indicative of a higher occurrence of metabolically suboptimal steps. The step length fluctuations were not predictive of changes in net MCOT. This indicates that the persistence of fluctuations likely does not function to reduce MCOT. Despite its potential to reduce MCOT, step length variations were not minimized at the PWS but were at fast walking speeds, where they may be reduced in order to mitigate the increased MCOT due to increased walking speed."
Climbing Stairs: 1 step or 2 "The present study findings indicate that (1) treadmill-calibrated heart rate recordings can be used as a valid alternative to respirometry to ascertain rate of energy expenditure during stair climbing; (2) two step climbing invokes a higher rate of energy expenditure; however, one step climbing is energetically more expensive in total over the entirety of a stairway. Therefore to expend the maximum number of calories when climbing a set of stairs the single-step strategy is better."
Symmetrical walking "walking with asymmetric step times required substantial metabolic costs above those imposed by non-preferred step times."
